# Universal Content Design Rules

Generalised from Sarah Winters / Content Design London principles. Written for any document, not just repo docs — a policy, a help article, a manual, a product page, an internal wiki, a form, anything.

`Content used to be something you read. Now it's something you use. Content design is about giving your audience what they want, when they need it, in the way they expect. — Sarah Winters`

The core move behind every rule below: ask "what content best meets this need?" before asking "how shall I write this?"

**1. Discovery: find the real need before writing**

1. Don't start from a blank page. Start from evidence of what the reader is actually trying to do.
2. Treat any recurring question, complaint, or confusion as a content need. The format varies by domain (support tickets, search logs, forum threads, reception desk questions, recurring meeting questions) but the signal is the same: if it keeps coming up, it isn't documented yet, or isn't documented where the reader looks.
3. Use the reader's own words, not your internal words. Find out what they actually type into a search box or say out loud, and use that as your vocabulary and your headings.
4. Identify the reader's existing mental model before you write. Decide, deliberately, whether your content should:
   - **reflect** it (confirm what they already correctly assume),
   - **rebut and re-educate** (say plainly that the obvious assumption is wrong, and why).
   Never leave a wrong assumption uncorrected by silence.
5. Discovery is "done" when new sessions or new evidence stop surfacing new needs — not after a fixed number of interviews.

**2. Frame the content before writing a word**

6. State whose need this serves: the reader's, the organisation's, or both. Don't write content that only serves the organisation and call it a user need.
7. Frame the content around a real situation, not an abstract feature:
   - Use **"As a [role], I want to [action], so that [goal]"** when the content serves several distinct kinds of reader.
   - Use **"When [situation], I want to [action], so I can [goal]"** when it serves one kind of reader across many situations.
   - Signal to switch from the first to the second: if every single frame you write starts with the same role, you don't have distinct readers — switch to situations.
8. Split compound needs into separate pieces of content. One need, one page (or one clearly separated section).
9. Never bake a solution into the stated need. "I need a calculator" is a solution; "I need to know what I owe" is the need.
10. Cut subjective, unmeasurable words from a stated need or goal — "quickly," "easily," "simply." They can't be tested.
11. Write a short, testable definition of "done" for the content before you write it. Don't put a specific solution into that definition either.
12. Map where the reader's journey actually starts — which is almost never your homepage, your manual's cover page, or your top-level menu. It's usually a search, an error, a sign someone else pointed them to, or a question they walked in with. Design the entry points that match where people really start, and link inward from there.

**3. Structure and headings**

13. Decide the shape and order of the content — what's a paragraph, what's a list, what's a table, what's a diagram — before you write sentences.
14. Order sections by frequency of need. The thing most readers need goes first. Rare cases go later, but stay findable.
15. Front-load every heading and title with the word or phrase the reader is scanning for. Put the most important term first, not buried at the end of the phrase.
16. Don't write headings as questions ("How do I…", "What is…"). Questions are slower to scan than a plain, front-loaded topic label.
17. Avoid clever, cute, or ambiguous headings the reader has to decode. A heading's only job is to be understood in under a second.
18. Don't build a separate FAQ as a dumping ground for things you didn't structure properly elsewhere. If a question is genuinely frequent, give the answer a real, findable, front-loaded heading inside the main structure. A small, curated set of true cross-cutting questions, backed by real recurring-question evidence, is fine — an unstructured catch-all is not.
19. Orient the reader in the first few seconds. The opening lines must confirm: what this is, and whether it's the right place for what they need.
20. Make cross-references and links descriptive of where they go ("see refund eligibility," not "click here" or "more information"). The reader should be able to tell, before clicking, whether continuing is worth it.

**4. Choose the right form of content**

21. Remember that content is not only words. Before defaulting to prose, ask whether the real answer is a table, a diagram, a decision tree, a checklist, a calculator, a worked example, or a single link.
22. Use a table for anything that's really a comparison or a set of parallel options or values.
23. Use a diagram for anything involving multiple steps, multiple actors, or a sequence over time.
24. Use a decision tree or short flow for "which option applies to me?" questions.
25. Use a numbered list only for steps that must happen in order.
26. Reserve prose for explanation, reasoning, and trade-offs — the "why," not data that's better shown as structure.
27. Anything copyable, quotable, or exact (a phrase to type, a code, a reference number, an exact form of words) should be set apart visually, not buried in a paragraph.

**5. Sentences and words**

28. Aim for an average sentence length of around 15 words.
29. Treat 25 words as the ceiling. Past that, split the sentence or convert it into a list.
30. Don't impose a fixed word count on a piece of content. Answer the need and stop — length should be driven by the need and the reading situation, not by a target.
31. Prefer short, common, everyday words over longer formal ones, except where precision genuinely requires the more specific word.
32. Define any specialist or field-specific term the first time you use it on a page — inline or via a link to a definition. Don't delete the term to "simplify"; deleting it removes precision. Simplify the sentence around it instead.
33. Expand any uncommon abbreviation or acronym in full on first use per page or section. Don't assume the reader saw the expansion earlier on a different page.
34. Don't use "i.e.", "e.g.", "etc." Spell out what you mean.
35. Clarity is not the same as terse, blunt, or boring. A document can have warmth and rhythm and still be plain and exact.
36. Use familiar, standard punctuation. Use sentence rhythm (short sentence, longer sentence) deliberately for pace and emphasis, not by accident.

**6. Lists and other formatting**

37. For an unordered list: give it a lead-in line ending in a colon, start each item lowercase, drop the end punctuation, keep every item grammatically parallel (all starting with a verb, or all nouns), and use 2–6 items.
38. For an ordered/numbered list (steps that must happen in sequence): start each item with a capital letter and end it with a full stop. No lead-in line needed.
39. Use sentence case for headings, titles, and buttons. Don't write headings in Title Case or ALL CAPS.
40. Capitalise only genuine proper nouns and official names. A specialist or technical term is not automatically a proper noun.

**7. Images, diagrams, and accessibility**

41. Use an image, icon, or diagram only when it genuinely conveys something words can't — spatial layout, a relationship between parts, a sequence.
42. Give every image and diagram a text equivalent (alt text or a short written summary) for anyone using a screen reader, and for the moment the image fails to load.
43. Check that diagrams and images still work in both light and dark display modes, and at different sizes.
44. Don't rely on emoji or colour alone to carry meaning. Anything conveyed visually needs a text-readable equivalent.

**8. Trust**

45. Treat correctness, currency, consistency, and basic spelling/grammar as trust infrastructure, not polish. A wrong or stale piece of content damages trust in everything else around it.
46. Any instruction, example, or step that doesn't actually work as written is a defect. Fix it or remove it — don't leave it live.
47. Resolve disagreements about what content should say with evidence (how often the confusion happens, how much it costs, what the actual reader behaviour shows) rather than with opinion or seniority.

**9. Collaboration and review**

48. When you need both accuracy and clarity, get them at the same time: write alongside the person who actually knows the subject, rather than writing alone and fact-checking after.
49. In that session, keep asking "what does that actually mean?" until the answer is in plain language — don't accept "everyone already knows this" as a reason to skip the explanation.
50. Run structured peer reviews of content itself (distinct from reviewing the underlying facts or system). Ground rules: critique the content, not the person who wrote it; no one has to defend their draft; phrase feedback as a shared standard ("our style puts the keyword first"), not a personal correction ("you always do this wrong"); share early, before anyone's attached to a draft.

**10. Lifecycle and maintenance**

51. Give every piece of content an owner, a review date, and a planned review cadence — set at the moment it's published, not added later as an afterthought.
52. Set an archive-or-delete point up front too. Nothing should be allowed to just sit indefinitely with no plan.
53. Define what success looks like for a piece of content (fewer repeated questions, faster task completion, fewer escalations) at the same time you define its owner and review date.
54. Audit on a schedule: oldest-since-review first, most-used first, or most-likely-out-of-date first. Treat content that's gone past its review date as a flag, not a low-priority backlog item.
55. When the thing the content describes changes, update the content in the same action — don't let the update become a separate, deferrable task.
